A Selection of Ancient, Traditional, and Modern Dishes

Discover the culinary heritage of South Asia and its deep-rooted traditions.

Ancient Foundational Heritage of Ayurvedic Herbal Practices

With culinary heritage based largely on ancient traditions, cultural practices, and religious beliefs in South Asia, including India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal, Bhutan, and the Maldives, the cuisine of this region is not just nutrition but an expression of identity, community, and spirituality. This article examines the historical, cultural, and health aspects of South Asian food identities, emphasizing their enduring importance.

South Asian culinary traditions draw on ancient practices such as Ayurveda, a holistic health system developed in India between 2500 and 500 B.C. It provides energy (Ferro-Well, 1977).

Staples like lentils, rice, and kojra were central to the menu, which included a variety of vegetables, fruits, and dairy products. Spices like turmeric, ginger, and cumin were used not only for their flavor but also for medicinal purposes. For example, turmeric was regarded for its anti-inflammatory properties, a practice that has been recognized by modern science (Rathi, Riddell & Worsley, 2018).

Religious Influences on Food Choices

Religious beliefs have played an important role in shaping South Asian cuisine. Vegetarianism is encouraged in Hinduism, and certain foods are considered either pure (sattvic) or impure (tama). Festivals like Navratri encourage fasting and consumption of sattvic foods such as fruits, nuts, and dairy products (Ferro-Luzzi, 1977).

Similarly, Islamic food laws in Pakistan and Bangladesh mandate the use of halal food, which ensures food is prepared following specific guidelines.
